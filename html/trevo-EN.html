01 "Back to UX/UI: new beginnings and the birth of TREEVO.

A new module, a new team, and a reunion with UX/UI

In the UX/UI module, everything started a bit turbulent for me because I was in a new class and needed to learn to work with unfamiliar people. But gradually, everything started flowing, and soon we formed a group with some super dedicated girls. The project would be entirely done in Figma, without direct programming, but with lots of prototyping and logic behind it. Going back to working with UX/UI, I realized there was a lot of theory I had forgotten — things I had learned at the beginning of my career, back in Bolivia — and that were now essential. Even though I was a bit lost at first, I fell in love with the field again and understood something important: it's not enough for me, as a designer, to understand the interface; the one who really needs to understand it is the user.

The birth of the Trevo idea: recycling can be fun

Our group's idea was to create a recycling app because we noticed that in Brazil there are different types of waste, specific rules, and a lot of misinformation — even I didn't know that fabrics couldn't be disposed of just anywhere. That's how TREVO was born, an app that worked like a "recycling Uber": you could schedule pickup from your own home or set a day to take your waste to a specific collection point. Inside the app there were educational articles, a rewards system, missions, and even an interactive mascot: a little plant that would grow as the user recycled — until it became a fully customizable melon. You could also add friends and compete to see who recycled the most, making the process fun and engaging.

02 Between components and creativity: how TREVOO. came to life

The unexpected reunion with "Figma programming": components

With the application flow underway, I faced a challenge that ended up becoming a passion: components. The girls were creating many separate screens, and I remembered — somewhat vaguely — that there was a more efficient way. That's when I reconnected with the programming logic I've always loved. Components were practically "mini-codes" within Figma: they allowed automation, organization, and drastically reduced the number of screens. The professor gave us the foundation, but I dove headfirst into studying and relearning. And then it happened: I fell completely in love with components. I started turning everything into a component, making the app cleaner, more functional, and modular. It was like programming, but within design — exactly the perfect balance I've always sought in my career.

The final result: an impeccable project and a wonderful group

Working with the girls was a wonderful experience. They had worked together before and had their own rhythm, so at first I was a bit lost trying to keep up. But soon I found my place on the team, contributing mainly to organization, components, and prototype logic. With all our joint effort, TREVO became a beautiful application, complete, coherent, and very well structured. I left the module proud not only of the project but of how much I grew by revisiting areas I had tried to "play dumb" about. In the end, I fell completely back in love with UX/UI and was certain that this is exactly what I want to pursue in my career. And so TREVO ends: with learning, teamwork, design... and a melon.

01 Where Loopi came from: from chaos to the first sketches

The initial chaos and the birth of the idea

It all started in a somewhat chaotic and hilarious way. My team and I had no idea what to create and, for some reason, we ended up analyzing physiotherapy chairs and even human vertebrae to think about foldable mechanisms. We were looking at references of toys like Snake Cube, imagining articulated shapes, when our professor walked by, looked at our screens and said: "Guys, it would be genius to make a children's chair in that format!". We looked at each other, kind of confused, and responded: "Yes, of course... that was exactly it!". And just like that, almost by accident, the starting point of the project was born.

From sketch to anthropometric study

From there, a storm of sketches began... many, many sketches. First we wanted to create a set of chairs like an amusement park, then organic shapes, then triangles â€” until we finally decided they would be triangular chairs. We thought the project was already solved by the second week, but the real chaos started when we realized we would need to justify absolutely everything: height, proportions, children's ergonomics and anthropometric studies (yes, that's what you were remembering!). The more we studied the ideal measurements for children aged 4 to 6, the more details emerged and the harder it became to keep the design functional without compromising play.

02 Construction, connections and pedagogical magic: the birth of Loopi

The mental torture of connections and the unexpected solution

Then came the most challenging phase: how the hell were the chairs going to connect? We tried joints, extra pieces, thick ropes, side mechanisms... and each professor said something different. At one point I lost my patience and said: "What if we just put a magnet and that's it?". Ironically, it was the first idea both professors approved at the same time. We did several tests and decided the magnets would be hidden inside the chairs. We modeled everything in Inventor software and made various prototypes with paper, cardboard and even that hardening clay-type putty. It was trial and error nonstop, but the triangular format worked because it allowed children to create paths, tunnels and various ways to play.

Transforming it into a pedagogical toy

The second part of the work was transforming the chair into something truly pedagogical. We divided the team: the boys stayed with the physical prototypes, and we took care of the design, name, functions and educational proposal. We created very subtle reliefs on the seat (letters, numbers and shapes), applied solid colors and added a signal system on the vertices using the magnet poles: "+" and "â€“", which helped children understand which pieces combined. This not only allowed building paths, but also taught basic notions of physics, logic and even math. We prepared an infographic with game suggestions, Montessori activities and ways to use the chairs without depending on screens. In the end, we presented the physical model and the 3D one, and it was, without a doubt, one of the best projects of the module â€” and the one that made me proudest.
